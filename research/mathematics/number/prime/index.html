Primes are fundamental, indivisible parts of natural numbers.

A prime is a natural number greater than 1 that cannot be divided by any other natural, apart from 1 and itself. Every natural number greater than 1 can be uniquely expressed as a product of prime numbers. This result is known as the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic.

There are infinitely many prime numbers.

Here's an argument by contradiction that proves the infinitude of primes:

Suppose there are finitely many primes. Let p be the product of all primes. Thus, p is divisible by all primes. The number p+1 is not divisible by any divisor of p. Therefore, p+1 is prime and does not divide p, which leads to a contradiction.

Distribution of Primes

The distribution of primes among the integers looks irregular, but it follows a predictable asymptotic pattern.

The Prime Number Theorem

It states that the number of primes less than a given number N is approximately equal to the natural logarithm of N. In other words, the average gap between consecutive prime numbers among the first N integers is roughly ln(N).

This theorem formalises the intuitive notion that primes become less common as they grow larger, by precisely quantifying the rate at which this occurs.

Riemann Hypothesis

This is one of the most famous unsolved problems in mathematics. It posits that the Riemann zeta function has its non-trivial zeros only at the negative even integers and complex numbers with real part 1/2.

The validity of the Riemann Hypothesis has strong implications for the distribution of primes. Specifically, it predicts tighter bounds on the error term in the Prime Number Theorem.
