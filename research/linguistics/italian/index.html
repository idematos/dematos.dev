Italian is a phonetic language.

A phonetic language has a consistent phoneme-grapheme correspondence, meaning words are pronounced as they are written.

Alphabet

Italian uses the Latin alphabet with 21 standard letters (A-Z, excluding J, K, W, X, and Y, which are used only in foreign words).

Phonology

Vowels

There are five vowel sounds (/a/, /e/, /ɛ/, /i/, /o/, /ɔ/, /u/) that can vary slightly depending on stress.

Consonants

Most consonants are pronounced similarly to their Latin equivalents. Double consonants are lengthened in duration (e.g., palla (ball) vs. pala (shovel)).

Morphology

Articles

Nouns are either masculine or feminine and are always preceded by a definite or indefinite article. Articles change depending on gender, number, and initial letter of the noun.

Singular Plural Example
il (m.) i il libro (the book) → i libri (the books)
la (f.) le la porta (the door) → le porte (the doors)
lo (m.) gli lo zio (the uncle) → gli zii (the uncles)
un (m.) — un cane (a dog)
una (f.) — una donna (a woman)

Verbs

Verbs are highly inflected, with endings changing based on tense, mood, and subject pronoun.
