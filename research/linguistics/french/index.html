French is one of the most geographically widespread languages.

An Indo-European language and part of the Romance language family, derived from Latin. It is an official language in 27 countries and serves as a de jure or de facto official, administrative, or cultural language in around 50 countries and territories.

Orthography

Words are written left to right, and its punctuation style largely mirrors English. Ligatures are sometimes used, such as œ in "œuvre" and æ in older texts like "cæur".

Accents

Accents modify pronunciation or meaning.

Accent Name Example
é accent aigu éléphant
è, à, ù accent grave très, où
ê, â, î, ô, û accent circonflexe forêt
ë, ï, ü tréma naïve

Cedilla (ç)

Softens the "c" before "a," "o," or "u," as in "façon".

Phonology

Vowels

a, e, i, o, u, y with variations (e.g., open/closed sounds: é vs. è)

Nasal vowels (vowels followed by "n" or "m")

e.g., "pain" [pɛ̃] (bread), "mon" [mɔ̃] (my).

French is syllable-timed, giving equal duration to syllables. Silent letters are frequent, especially at the end of words (-e, -s, -t, -d), contributing to liaison (linking sounds between words).

Examples of liaison:

"Les amis" → [lez‿ami]

"Nous avons" → [nuz‿avɔ̃]

Grammar

Highly inflected, with gendered nouns, adjective agreement, and verb conjugation across multiple tenses and moods.

Gender and Number

Nouns

Masculine or feminine (e.g., "le chat" (the male cat) / "la chatte" (the female cat)).

Articles

Definite (le, la, les), indefinite (un, une, des), partitive (du, de la, des).

Adjectives

Must agree in gender and number (e.g., "grand" → "grande", "grands", "grandes").

Verbs

Conjugate in three major groups based on their infinitive endings: -er, -ir, -re. Conjugation varies by tense, mood, and person.

Present tense: "Je mange" (I eat).

Past tense: "J'ai mangé" (I ate).

Future tense: "Je mangerai" (I will eat).

Subjunctive: "Il faut que je mange" (It is necessary that I eat).

Auxiliary verbs (avoir and être) form compound tenses like passé composé.

Vocabulary

French shares significant vocabulary with English due to historical connections, particularly Norman French's influence on Old English. Many French words stem from Latin roots. Word formation relies heavily on affixes:

Prefixes (re-, in-, dé-): "refaire" (redo), "inactif" (inactive).

Suffixes (-ment, -able): "rapidement" (quickly), "adorable" (adorable).
