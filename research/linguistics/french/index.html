French is syllable-timed.

Syllable-timed languages have equal duration to syllables.

Orthography

Ligatures are sometimes used, such as œ in "œuvre" and æ in older texts like "cæur".

Accents

Accents modify pronunciation or meaning.

Accent Name Example
é accent aigu éléphant
è, à, ù accent grave très, où
ê, â, î, ô, û accent circonflexe forêt
ë, ï, ü tréma naïve

Cedilla (ç)

Softens the "c" before "a," "o," or "u," as in "façon".

Phonology

Vowels

a, e, i, o, u, y with variations (e.g., open/closed sounds: é vs. è)

Nasal vowels (vowels followed by "n" or "m")

e.g., "pain" [pɛ̃] (bread), "mon" [mɔ̃] (my).

Silent Letters

Used especially at the end of words (-e, -s, -t, -d), creating liaison (linking sounds between words).

"Les amis" → [lez‿ami]

"Nous avons" → [nuz‿avɔ̃]

Grammar

Highly inflected, with gendered nouns, adjective agreement, and verb conjugation across multiple tenses and moods.

Gender and Number

Nouns

Masculine or feminine (e.g., "le chat" (the male cat) / "la chatte" (the female cat)).

Articles

Definite (le, la, les), indefinite (un, une, des), partitive (du, de la, des).

Adjectives

Must agree in gender and number (e.g., "grand" → "grande", "grands", "grandes").

Verbs

Conjugate in three major groups based on their infinitive endings: -er, -ir, -re. Conjugation varies by tense, mood, and person.

Present tense: "Je mange" (I eat).

Past tense: "J'ai mangé" (I ate).

Future tense: "Je mangerai" (I will eat).

Auxiliary verbs (avoir and être) form compound tenses like passé composé.

Vocabulary

Many words stem from Latin roots. Word formation relies heavily on affixes:

Prefixes (re-, in-, dé-): "refaire" (redo), "inactif" (inactive).

Suffixes (-ment, -able): "rapidement" (quickly), "adorable" (adorable).
