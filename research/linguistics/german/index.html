German is the most widely spoken native language within the European Union.

An Indo-European language belonging to the West Germanic branch, it is closely related to English and Dutch. German has a rich morphology, complex syntax, and extensive vocabulary formed through compounding and derivation.

Morphology

A single affix often carries multiple grammatical meanings, such as tense, number, and case. Nouns are highly inflected and are always capitalised.

Gender and Case

Nouns are assigned one of three grammatical genders: masculine, feminine, or neuter. They also decline according to four cases: nominative, accusative, dative, and genitive. Articles, adjectives, and pronouns are inflected to match the case, number, and gender of the noun.

Gender Definite Article Indefinite Article Example
Masculine der ein der Hund (the dog)
Feminine die eine die Katze (the cat)
Neuter das ein das Haus (the house)

Syntax

German follows the Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) order in main clauses but shifts to Subject-Object-Verb (SOV) in subordinate clauses. Verbs often appear in the second position in main clauses and at the end of subordinate clauses, making word order highly flexible.

Main Clause: Ich kaufe ein Buch (I buy a book).

Subordinate Clause: Ich weiß, dass er ein Buch kauft (I know that he is buying a book).

Compound Words

One of German's hallmark features is its ability to create long compound words that convey highly specific meanings. These words often combine multiple nouns into a single entity.

Donaudampfschifffahrtsgesellschaftskapitän (Danube steamship company captain).

Handschuh (literally "hand shoe", meaning glove).

Phonology

Pronunciation is relatively consistent, with each letter or combination of letters generally corresponding to specific sounds.

  • Umlauts (Ä, Ö, Ü) modify the vowels a, o, and u, changing their pronunciation.

  • The Eszett (ß) represents a sharp "s" sound.

Stress is typically placed on the first syllable of a word, although compound words are stressed on their primary root.

Verbs

Verbs are conjugated based on person, number, tense, and mood. Strong verbs change their stem vowels, while weak verbs follow regular conjugation patterns. Modal verbs are also an important part of the grammar, expressing necessity, permission, or ability.

Pronoun Gehen (to go) Conjugation
ich (I) gehe (I go)
du (you) gehst (you go)
er/sie/es (he/she/it) geht (he/she/it goes)
wir (we) gehen (we go)
ihr (you all) geht (you all go)
sie (they) gehen (they go)

Orthography

German uses the Latin script with additional diacritical marks (Ä, Ö, Ü, ß). Punctuation rules are strict and closely tied to sentence structure.

Quotation Marks

It is used „…“ or «…» for direct quotes.

Commas

Used extensively to separate subordinate clauses.

Ich denke, dass er kommt (I think that he is coming).
