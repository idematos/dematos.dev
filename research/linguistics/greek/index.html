Φιλοξενία

0241229

Greek is one of the oldest documented languages.

With a history spanning over 3400 years, it is the cornerstone of Western civilization and philosophy. It is the official language of Greece and Cyprus and is written using its own alphabet.

Morphology

Words are written from left to right, and employ accents and diacritics to indicate stress and, in older texts, breathing marks.

Alphabet

The Greek alphabet consists of 24 letters and has been the foundation of many modern scripts, including Latin and Cyrillic.

Phonology

Vowels

Vowel sounds (/a/, /e/, /i/, /o/, /u/) are written with multiple graphemes:

  • η, ι, υ, ει, οι → all pronounced /i/.

  • ο, ω → pronounced /o/.

Consonants

There are both voiced and unvoiced stops, fricatives, and the characteristic dental fricatives (/θ/ and /ð/).

Stress

Modern words are stressed on one of the last three syllables. The stressed syllable is marked with an acute accent (´).

Morphology

Articles

Nouns are marked by gender (masculine, feminine, neuter) and always take a definite or indefinite article.

Singular (Definite) Plural (Definite) Example
ο (m.) οι (m.) ο άνθρωπος ("the man") / οι άνθρωποι ("the men")
η (f.) οι (f.) η γυναίκα ("the woman") / οι γυναίκες ("the women")
το (n.) τα (n.) το σπίτι ("the house") / τα σπίτια ("the houses")

Verbs

Verbs are conjugated for tense, mood, aspect, voice, and person. Verbs have a rich morphology, often including prefixes and suffixes.

Pronoun γράφω (to write)
Εγώ (I) Γράφω (I write)
Εσύ (You) Γράφεις (You write)
Αυτός/Αυτή (He/She) Γράφει (He/She writes)
Εμείς (We) Γράφουμε (We write)
Εσείς (You all) Γράφετε (You all write)
Αυτοί (They) Γράφουν (They write)

Derivations

Suffixes and prefixes indicate grammatical relationships and derivations.

Plurals

Masculine and neuter nouns often end in -ος in singular and -οι/-α in plural (άνθρωπος (man) → άνθρωποι (men)).

Diminutives

-άκι: e.g., σπίτι (house) → σπιτάκι (little house).

Augmentatives

-αρα: e.g., σπιταρα (big house).

Diacritics

Modern Greek uses fewer diacritics than Ancient Greek.

  • Acute accent (´): Marks the stressed syllable (e.g., καφές (coffee)).

  • Diaeresis (¨): Indicates that two vowels should be pronounced separately (e.g., αϊτός (eagle))

  • Combining diacritics (rare): Used in older polytonic texts, including breathing marks (῾, ᾿).
