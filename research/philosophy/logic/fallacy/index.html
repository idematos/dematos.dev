Logical fallacies are errors in reasoning.

They can obscure the truth, manipulate opinions, and lead to poor outcomes.

  • Ad Hominem: This occurs when one attacks the person making an argument rather than addressing the argument itself. This fallacy shifts the focus from the issue at hand to irrelevant personal characteristics.

  • Straw Man: This involves misrepresenting an argument to make it easier to attack, often by exaggerating or distorting it.

  • False Dilemma: This occurs when an argument presents only two options, ignoring other possibilities that might exist.

  • Circular Reasoning: This happens when the conclusion of an argument is assumed within its premises.

  • Slippery Slope: This argues that a specific action will inevitably lead to a chain of undesirable events, without evidence to support such a progression.

  • Hasty Generalization: This involves drawing a conclusion based on insufficient or unrepresentative evidence.

  • Appeal to Authority: This occurs when someone claims an argument is valid simply because an authority figure supports it, without addressing the merits of the argument.

  • Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc (False Cause): This assumes that because one event follows another, the first must have caused the second.

  • Red Herring: This involves diverting attention from the main issue by introducing an irrelevant topic.

  • Bandwagon Appeal: This argues that something is true or good simply because many people believe it or do it.
